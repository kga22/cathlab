RIGHT HEART CATHETERIZATION

1

What Happens

A long, thin catheter - known as a Pulmonary Artery or Swan-Ganz catheter - is inserted into either the internal jugular vein, the brachial/cephalic/median cubital vein, or the femoral vein and threaded up into the right atrium, the right ventricle, and finally into the pulmonary artery. Note that the order of where the catheter is advanced is subject to physician preference. As the catheter is advanced through each chamber, waveforms and pressures are recorded. Blood samples are also drawn from the pulmonary artery to measure oximetry and calculate cardiac output. It is important to use mixed venous blood as it captures blood before getting re-oxygenated.

A right heart catheterization maybe done as a stand-alone procedure, or it maybe done in conjunction with a left heart catheterization or an endomyocardial biopsy, depending on the patient's clinical presentation. When it is done as an outpatient procedure, the catheter is removed at the conclusion of the procedure. However, a PA catheter may also be left in place to monitor and guide therapy for those who are inpatient.

6

Considerations

Before the case starts, make sure to enter the patient's height, weight, hgb, and hct into Xper as the computer uses that information to calculate CO and CI.

Zero transducer to atmospheric pressure.

PA Distal port (yellow): fluid will exit at the tip of the catheter, in the pulmonary artery. Used to draw blood for mixed SvO2 samples.

CVP Proximal port (blue): fluid will exit below the 30cm mark. Sits in the right atrium. For measurement of CVP, RAP, and infusion of fluids, and injection for measurement of CO.

Venous Infusion port (white): fluid will exit above the 30cm mark. Can be used for fluids and medications.

Balloon port (red): to connect syringe to inflate air.

Balloon is inflated when advancing.

Balloon is deflated when withdrawing.

Never lock the syringe when the balloon is inflated - as this presents a risk for pulmonary infarct or occlusion. Tthe red line on the syringe will be straight when it's open. The red line will not be straight when the syringe is locked).

7

Important Hemodynamic Parameters

Right Atrial Pressure (RAP): the blood pressure in the right atrium of the heart. Reflects the amount of blood returning to the heart, and the ability of the heart to pump blood back into the arterial system.

Right Ventricular Pressure (RVP): the blood pressure in the right ventricle. Can estimate pulmonary artery pressure.

Pulmonary Artery Pressure (PAP): measures blood pressure found in the main pulmonary artery. Can definitively diagnose pulmonary hypertension.

Pulmonary Capillary Wedge Pressure (PCWP): this is also known as PWP, PAWP, or PAOP. Provides an indirect estimate of left atrial pressure, the severity of left ventricular failure, and the degree of mitral valve stenosis. Both of these conditions elevate LAP and therefore PCWP.

Cardiac Output (CO): the volume of blood being pumped by the heart per minute. Predicts oxygen delivery to cells.

Cardiac Index (CI): cardiac output that corrects for the patient's body surface area (BSA).

Central Venous Pressure: the pressure in the thoracic vena cava near the right atrium.

Pulmonary Vascular Resistance: the right ventricle ejects blood through the pulmonic valve against the low pressure of the pulmonary circulation.

Systemic Vascular Resistance: the left ventricle ejects blood through the aortic valve against the high pressure of the systemic circulation.

Calculating Cardiac Output

Fick's: an indirect measurement of oxygen consumption and CO using whole body oxygen consumption (VO2), mixed venous oxygenation (CvO2), and arterial oxygen concentrations (CaO2), expressed as CO = VO2/(CaO2 â CvO2. As long as the appropriate values are entered into the computer, the computer calculates this automatically.

Thermodilution: A cold saline solution is manually injected by the physician with a syringe into the right atrium from a proximal catheter port during the end of expiration. The solution mixes with and cools the blood as it passes through the ventricle and into the pulmonary artery. The blood temperature is measured by a thermistor at the catheter tip within the pulmonary artery. The computer measures the change in blood temperature as it flows over the thermistor.
